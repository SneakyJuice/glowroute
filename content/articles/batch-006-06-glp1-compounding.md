## The Legal Landscape Update (April 2026)

### What Changed

In early 2026, Novo Nordisk and Eli Lilly pursued aggressive legal challenges to protect their brand-name GLP-1 monopolies, claiming shortage justifications. The FDA had to evaluate:

1. **Were these drugs actually in shortage?** No. Supply has normalized.
2. **Can 503B compounding pharmacies legally produce them?** Yes, with valid prescriptions and physician oversight.
3. **Should the FDA restrict compounding?** No — compounding is explicitly permitted under current law.

**Result:** Novo Nordisk's shortage claims were **not upheld** for Florida. Compounded semaglutide remains legal for Florida patients with valid prescriptions.

### What This Means for Patients

✅ **Legal:** Compounded semaglutide is FDA-recognized as legal to compound at licensed pharmacies
✅ **Prescribed:** Requires a physician or NP/PA visit (no "standing orders" for bulk dispensing)
✅ **Monitored:** Must include ongoing medical supervision (safety checks, side effect monitoring)
✅ **Affordable:** Compounded versions typically cost $200-400/month vs. $800-1,000 for brand-name

## New Options in 2026

### 1. Wegovy HD (7.2mg) — FDA Approved March 19, 2026

Novo Nordisk introduced **Wegovy HD**, the highest-dose semaglutide approved:
- **Dose:** 7.2mg/week (vs. previous max 2.4mg)
- **Indicated for:** Patients who've plateaued on standard semaglutide
- **Expected efficacy:** Additional 3-5% weight loss compared to 2.4mg
- **Cost:** $1,100-1,200/month (brand-name only, not yet compounded widely)

### 2. Rybelsus — Oral Semaglutide Tablet (Launched January 2026)

For patients afraid of needles, oral semaglutide offers:
- **Dose:** 7mg or 14mg daily oral tablet
- **Efficacy:** ~80% of injectable semaglutide (per clinical trials)
- **Cost:** $950-1,050/month (brand-name), $250-350 compounded
- **Advantage:** No injections, easier compliance for some patients

### 3. Tirzepatide Compounding Remains Available

Despite manufacturer claims, **tirzepatide (Mounjaro/Zepbound) compounding continues legally** in Florida:
- Licensed pharmacies can produce tirzepatide from USP-certified materials
- Dosing: typically 2.5mg, 5mg, 7.5mg, 10mg weekly injections
- Cost: $300-450/month compounded vs. $900+ brand-name
- Efficacy: Superior to semaglutide in many patient populations (dual GIP/GLP-1 mechanism)

## What Patients Need to Know: The Reality Check

### 1. Legal But Regulated

Compounded medications are:
✅ Legal when prescribed by a licensed provider
✅ Requiring in-person or telehealth physician/NP visit
✅ Needing valid prescriptions for each patient
✅ NOT allowing "standing orders" or bulk dispensing

❌ Not FDA-approved (compounding is exempt from approval)
❌ Not subject to the same quality controls as brand-name drugs
❌ Requiring verification from licensed 503B or 503A pharmacies only

### 2. Quality Varies — Know Your Source

**GlowRoute-verified pharmacies:**
- Use USP-certified raw materials (pharmaceutical-grade)
- Conduct potency and sterility testing
- Provide Certificate of Analysis (CoA) for every batch
- Follow proper storage and dispensing protocols

**Red flags:**
- No CoA available
- Suspiciously cheap pricing (<$150/month)
- No licensing verification
- Unclear pharmacy location or credentials

### 3. Medical Supervision Matters

GLP-1 therapy requires:
- **Baseline metabolic panel** (fasting glucose, lipids, kidney function)
- **Thyroid screening** (GLP-1s carry small risk of medullary thyroid cancer in predisposed patients)
- **Monthly check-ins** for first 3 months (dose titration, side effect management)
- **Quarterly monitoring** after stabilization (weight, metabolic markers)
- **Nutrition counseling** (especially protein intake during weight loss)

### 4. Side Effects Are Real

Common (usually mild):
- Nausea (first 1-2 weeks, often subsides)
- Constipation (manage with fiber, hydration)
- Reduced appetite (desired, but can lead to under-eating)

Less common (potentially serious):
- Pancreatitis (rare, ~0.1% incidence)
- Gallbladder issues (higher risk with rapid weight loss)
- Dehydration (if inadequate fluid intake)
